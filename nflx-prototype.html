Feedback Time!

Creatively Blocked. Send Help.

I had one day until the product would be seen by students. Finding myself stuck knowing how much work I had left to do, I sought out help and feedback from my team.

Me: "Is this prototype enough for students to test?"

My team: "Not really." üò´

The Feedback: I was doing too much ...and not enough.

Too much: Real Content

I focused too much on having realistic content in the prototype. This was time-consuming, not entirely necessary, and out of scope for our timeline

Not enough: Missing Feature to Test

Students couldn't realistically make usability test scenarios from the prototype I created. We needed to add a feature that students could test

No Time? No Sweat. (Thanks, Components)

Thankfully, my design system made it easy to swap components in the layout using Variants.

Feature Inspiration: Stop the Endless Scroll

I wanted to replace the homework's "Play Something" button testing scenario with something more novel to the Netflix site.

I decided to empathize with a common experience on Netflix: scrolling for lengths of time trying to find something to watch, then watching Seinfeld for the 1000th time.

Solution: "Limit My Options" button

The"Limit My Options" button empathizes with the average person's short term memory capacity (about 7 items at a time) and provides users with a limited selection of shows to watch to reduce decision overwhelm.

Final Prototype

User:"...is this usable?"

The instructional staff reported student confusion around how to test this prototype. The staff itself expressed confusion on why the prototype was low-fidelity.

Our Immediate Solution: "Yes, it's usable"

Because students only had a week to complete the homework with no time to slow down, our best and most efficient solution for this cohort was to encourage the staff that the prototype is indeed testable.

We reminded them that lo-fi prototypes are often tested for usability in the UserResearch world.

Next Step: Investigate the Issue

I want to improve this user experience. If given the opportunity, my plan would be to investigate the users' feelings further with the following research methods:

  • Conduct usability tests on the homework assignment with students from the next cohort,
  • Observe reactions to the homework/prototype from within the classroom, and/or
  • Interview the original cohort's instructional staff to understand what exactly about the prototype was confusing and how much of a blocker it actually was.
